# Week 36, Day 7

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby is now considered "late preterm" if born today — and would likely do very well, perhaps needing just brief observation. Every day from here adds to brain development, fat stores, and lung maturity. The sucking reflex is getting stronger, which is great news for feeding after birth. Baby's sleep-wake cycles are becoming more defined, with longer periods of wakefulness.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
The final weeks can feel like time has slowed to a crawl. Your partner might be experiencing a deep tiredness that goes beyond physical — it's the accumulation of months of discomfort, anticipation, and hormonal changes. She might cry more easily, get frustrated with small things, or feel a wave of fierce protectiveness over the baby. All of this is her body and brain preparing for motherhood.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Small, protein-rich snacks before bed can help stabilize blood sugar overnight and reduce those middle-of-the-night hunger pangs. Ideas: a small bowl of cottage cheese with fruit, a hard-boiled egg, or a handful of almonds with a few dates. Keeping blood sugar steady also helps with mood regulation.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Today's exercise: rest and restore. A warm (not hot) Epsom salt bath can soothe sore muscles and joints, reduce swelling, and provide a rare moment of weightless comfort. Add a few drops of lavender essential oil for relaxation. Keep the water temperature below 100°F (38°C), and limit bathing to 15-20 minutes.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
You can't breastfeed, but you can absolutely support breastfeeding. Here's how to be a breastfeeding MVP:
- **Bring water and snacks** to your partner every time she nurses (breastfeeding burns 500+ calories a day and makes you incredibly thirsty)
- **Learn basic latch signs** — does baby's mouth cover most of the areola? Can you hear swallowing?
- **Handle burping duty** after feeds
- **Manage the logistics** — set up a nursing station with pillows, nipple cream, burp cloths, and water
- **Be patient** — breastfeeding doesn't always come naturally. It's a learned skill for both mom and baby. It can take days or weeks to establish
- **Don't panic** if it's hard — that's what lactation consultants are for. Have one's number saved in your phone

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What's our feeding plan? Breast, formula, or combination? And how do we feel about it?" This should be a judgment-free conversation. Fed is best, and the choice belongs to your partner with your support.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Newborns can smell their mother's breast milk and will turn toward it. In studies, babies placed between two breast pads — one from their mother and one from a stranger — consistently turn toward their own mother's scent. Your baby already knows her.

## 🔮 After-Birth Preview: Postpartum Recovery Timeline
Here's a realistic timeline of your partner's recovery:

**Days 1-3:**
- Uterine contractions (especially during breastfeeding) as the uterus shrinks
- Heavy bleeding (lochia) — like a very heavy period
- Perineal soreness (if vaginal birth) — ice packs, peri bottle, and sitz baths help
- Breast engorgement when milk comes in (typically day 3-5) — can be very painful
- Extreme exhaustion
- Hormone crash — expect tears, mood swings, and overwhelm

**Weeks 1-2:**
- Bleeding continues but lightens gradually
- Stitches healing (perineal or cesarean incision)
- Night sweats as the body sheds excess fluid
- Possible difficulty with bowel movements (stool softeners are common)
- Baby blues peak around day 5 and typically resolve by week 2

**Weeks 3-6:**
- Energy slowly returns (very slowly)
- Bleeding tapers off
- 6-week postpartum check is the milestone for clearance to resume exercise and intimacy
- Body still looks and feels very different from pre-pregnancy
- Hair loss may begin (peaks around 3-4 months)

**Your role in all of this:** Be her teammate. Manage the household, protect her rest, and remind her that recovery isn't linear — some days will feel great, others will feel like a setback. Both are normal.

## 🙏 Rest & Gratitude
Three weeks to go. Take a moment to acknowledge how far you've both come. From that first positive test to now — you've navigated appointments, decisions, fears, and so much growth. You're almost there. Rest well tonight.
