# Week 40, Day 5

## 👶 Baby Development
Right now, your baby is swallowing amniotic fluid and producing urine — a full cycle that helps keep the amniotic fluid volume balanced. Baby's sucking reflex is strong and ready. Interestingly, some babies are even born with blisters on their hands or lips from sucking in the womb — completely harmless and they disappear quickly. The digestive system is primed and ready for its first real meal: colostrum, the thick, golden "liquid gold" your partner's body has been preparing.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Emotionally, your partner might be in a strange limbo. Every twinge makes her think "is this it?" and every uneventful hour brings a wave of impatience. She might feel weepy, restless, or strangely calm. All of it is normal. Physically, the increased pelvic pressure means she might be waddling more than walking, and sleeping in any position for longer than 45 minutes might feel impossible. If she's up for it, warm baths can be incredibly soothing.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Dates and spicy food might not actually induce labor (despite the myths), but there's one food-related tip that does have some evidence: a full, satisfying meal during early labor. If contractions start but are still mild and far apart, encourage your partner to eat something substantial. A bowl of pasta, toast with eggs, a hearty soup — whatever sounds good. Once active labor kicks in, she likely won't want to eat, and she'll need those energy reserves. Think of it as carb-loading before a marathon, because that's essentially what labor is.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
This close to birth, the best "exercise" might be resting. Seriously. If your partner has been pushing through discomfort to stay active, today is a good day to give her permission to stop. Sleep, lie on the couch, take a bath, read a book. Her body needs energy reserves for labor, and exhaustion going in makes everything harder. A well-rested body labors more efficiently than a depleted one.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
When you arrive at the hospital, you become your partner's advocate. That means knowing her birth plan and being ready to communicate it to the medical team — calmly and respectfully. It also means being flexible: if the doctor recommends something that wasn't in the plan, ask questions ("Can you explain why this is necessary? What are the alternatives?"), relay the information to your partner, and support HER decision. You're the bridge between her and the medical team. That's a powerful and important role.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What's the first thing you want to say to our baby?" There's no wrong answer. Some parents say hello, some cry, some laugh, some just stare in awe. Knowing what your partner imagines for that moment — and sharing your own vision — makes it even more special when it arrives.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Babies can smell their mother within minutes of birth and will actually turn their head toward a cloth soaked in their mother's breast milk, even over a cloth soaked in another mother's milk. This isn't learned — it's hardwired. Your baby already knows who mom is.

## 🤔 Yesterday's Answer
**What happens during the first hour after birth?**

**For Baby:**
- Immediately after delivery, baby is typically placed on mom's bare chest (if all is well). The medical team will quickly assess baby's color, breathing, and muscle tone (Apgar score at 1 minute and 5 minutes).
- Baby may be surprisingly alert, blinking, looking around, even making small crawling movements toward the breast.
- The umbilical cord is clamped and cut (you may be offered the chance to cut it — it's totally okay to say yes or no).
- Within the first hour, baby may latch and breastfeed for the first time. Don't worry if it doesn't happen right away — it can take practice.

**For Mom:**
- The placenta will be delivered (Stage 3 of labor). The doctor will examine it to make sure it's complete.
- She'll be assessed for any tearing and may receive stitches (with local anesthesia).
- Nurses will check her bleeding and press on her abdomen to make sure the uterus is contracting down (this is uncomfortable but important).
- A flood of hormones — oxytocin, endorphins, adrenaline — will make everything feel surreal. She might shake, feel cold, or feel euphoric.

**For Dad:**
- Be present. Put the phone down for a few minutes and just be in this moment.
- Take photos, but not the whole time. Your memory of this hour matters too.
- Tell your partner she's incredible (because she is).
- If asked, do skin-to-skin yourself. Your baby needs your warmth and voice too.
- Manage communication: send one group text to close family, then put the phone away. Everyone else can wait.

## 🧠 Mental Health Check-In
The days around the due date can be surprisingly hard emotionally. There's a strange grief that some partners feel — the end of a chapter, the loss of a simpler life, fear of the unknown. If you're feeling this, you're not broken or ungrateful. You're human. Becoming a parent is one of the biggest identity shifts a person goes through, and it's okay to have complicated feelings about it. The love will come — and it'll be bigger than anything you've ever felt. It just might take a minute.

## 💕 Intimacy Corner
Right now, the most intimate thing you can do is be completely present with your partner. Look her in the eyes. Really listen when she talks. Touch her — not necessarily sexually, but with intention. Hold her hand, rub her feet, stroke her hair. In the chaos of anticipation, these small physical connections remind both of you that at the center of everything — the hospital bags, the birth plans, the nursery — is the two of you. And that's what this baby is coming home to.
