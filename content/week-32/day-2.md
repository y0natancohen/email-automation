# Week 32, Day 2

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby's toenails are now visible and growing! While fingernails have been developed for a while, the toenails are catching up. The bones throughout the body are continuing to harden — a process called ossification. Calcium is being deposited rapidly, strengthening the skeleton. The skull, however, remains soft and flexible on purpose. The skull bones have gaps between them (called fontanelles, or "soft spots") that allow the head to mold during birth. These fontanelles will gradually close over the first 18 months of life.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Your partner might be surprised to discover that her rib cage has actually expanded. During pregnancy, the ribs spread to accommodate the growing uterus and provide more lung space. This expansion can be uncomfortable, and she might notice that bras and shirts feel tighter around the ribcage even if the belly isn't bigger there. Some women find that a soft, stretchy bra without underwire is the most comfortable option. The rib expansion usually resolves after birth, though it can take a few months.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Your partner should be eating about 450 extra calories per day now — but quality matters more than quantity. Here's what 450 healthy calories looks like:
- An apple with 2 tablespoons of peanut butter + a glass of milk (about 400 cal)
- A turkey and avocado sandwich on whole grain bread (about 450 cal)
- Greek yogurt with granola and berries + a handful of almonds (about 430 cal)

It's not much food — but it's enough to fuel your baby's rapid growth.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
**Focus: Preparing for Pushing**
The muscles used for pushing during delivery are the same ones used for... well, going to the bathroom. Deep squats and bearing-down exercises can help prepare:
- Supported deep squat: hold onto a doorframe or partner's hands, lower into a deep squat, hold for 15-30 seconds
- Practice exhaling while engaging the core (simulating a push without actually pushing)
- Pelvic floor relaxation: instead of clenching (like a Kegel), practice actively releasing and relaxing the pelvic floor

Understanding how to relax the pelvic floor is just as important as knowing how to tighten it.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Learn about the fontanelles (soft spots on the baby's head). After birth, you'll notice one on the top of the head (anterior fontanelle) and a smaller one at the back (posterior fontanelle). These can feel alarming — they're soft, and you might even see them pulsing with the heartbeat. But they're completely normal and protected by a tough membrane. You won't hurt the baby by gently washing their hair or touching these areas. The anterior fontanelle typically closes between 12-18 months, and the posterior one closes within the first few months.

## 🤔 Weekly Q&A — Answer
**Yesterday's question:** What should we know about C-sections?

**Answer:** About 20-25% of births in Israel are cesarean sections. There are two types:

**Planned (Elective) C-sections** happen when:
- The baby is breech and won't turn
- There are certain medical conditions (placenta previa, some maternal health issues)
- Multiple babies (sometimes)
- Your partner or doctor decides it's the safest option for specific reasons

**Emergency C-sections** happen when:
- The baby shows signs of distress during labor
- Labor stalls and doesn't progress
- The umbilical cord prolapses
- Unexpected complications arise

What to know: A C-section is a major abdominal surgery. Recovery takes longer than vaginal birth (typically 4-6 weeks). Your partner won't be able to drive, lift heavy things, or do strenuous activity during recovery. She'll need more help at home. During the surgery, you'll typically be in the operating room with her (seated by her head), and you can often do skin-to-skin contact in the OR after the baby is delivered. A C-section is not a "failure" — it's a safe, valid way of bringing your baby into the world. Include C-section preferences in your birth plan, just in case.
