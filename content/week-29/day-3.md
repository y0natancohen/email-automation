# Week 29, Day 3

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby is running out of room! The amniotic sac that once felt like a swimming pool is now more like a snug sleeping bag. This is why movements have shifted from dramatic kicks to rolling, stretching, and squirming. Your baby is still plenty active, though — just in a more contained way. Limbs might poke out visibly against your partner's belly, creating those alien-like bumps and ripples that are equal parts fascinating and surreal.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
As the belly grows, everyday tasks become more challenging. Your partner might struggle with tying shoes, picking things up from the floor, or even finding a comfortable way to sit at a desk. Her center of gravity has shifted, making her more prone to bumps and fumbles. Carpal tunnel syndrome can also appear during pregnancy — tingling, numbness, or weakness in the hands, especially at night. This is caused by fluid retention putting pressure on the nerves in the wrist. Wrist splints worn at night can help.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Complex carbohydrates are essential for sustained energy in the third trimester. Unlike simple sugars that cause energy spikes and crashes, complex carbs release energy slowly. Great options: brown rice, sweet potatoes, oats, whole grain bread, and quinoa. A sweet potato topped with black beans, salsa, and a squeeze of lime is a quick, satisfying meal that provides complex carbs, protein, and fiber all in one.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Arm circles and shoulder rolls might seem simple, but they're surprisingly effective for relieving upper back and neck tension — common in the third trimester as posture changes. Try this:
- 10 large arm circles forward, 10 backward
- 10 shoulder rolls forward, 10 backward
- Interlace fingers behind the back, squeeze shoulder blades together, lift arms slightly, hold 10 seconds

Do this a couple of times a day, especially if your partner sits at a desk.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Start building your confidence for the delivery room. Watch some positive birth videos together (with your partner's agreement). Not the dramatic reality TV kind — look for calm, supported birth experiences that show partners being helpful. Seeing real births can reduce anxiety and help you visualize your role. If videos aren't your thing, read birth stories from other dads. Knowing what to expect is the best preparation.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"When the baby is here, who do you think should be our 'call first' people? Let's make a list of who to notify and in what order."

## 🎉 Fun Fact
At 29 weeks, your baby has fully developed taste buds — about 10,000 of them, actually more than an adult has! Some of these will fade after birth. But right now, your baby can taste a range of flavors through the amniotic fluid, from garlic to vanilla to whatever your partner had for dinner last night.

## 🛏️ Sleep Tip
If nighttime leg cramps are waking your partner up, here's a tip: flex the foot (pull toes toward the shin) rather than pointing it. Pointing the toes can actually trigger a cramp, while flexing stretches the calf muscle and provides relief. Gentle calf stretches before bed, staying hydrated, and eating magnesium-rich foods (bananas, dark chocolate, nuts) can also help prevent cramps from happening in the first place.

## 💼 Workplace Tip
If your partner is working from home or at an office, ergonomics become even more important now. Her chair should support her lower back, her feet should rest flat on the floor (or on a footrest), and the computer screen should be at eye level. A small cushion behind the lower back can help maintain the natural curve of the spine. She should also get up and move at least once every hour — even just a walk to the kitchen and back helps prevent stiffness and promotes circulation.
