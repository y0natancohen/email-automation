# Week 39, Day 3

## 👶 Baby Development
Baby's liver has been storing iron for the past several weeks, building up reserves that will last through the first few months of life. This is important because breast milk, while perfect in almost every way, is relatively low in iron — so baby needs this stockpile. The adrenal glands are also ramping up, producing cortisol that will help baby's lungs work independently after birth. Your little one is basically doing final systems checks.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Nesting instinct may be hitting hard. If your partner suddenly has an irresistible urge to organize the baby's dresser for the third time, deep-clean the kitchen, or rearrange furniture at 11 PM — that's nesting. It's a real biological drive, and while it can be productive, remind her to rest too. Her body is about to do something extraordinary and needs energy reserves. She might also notice more frequent or intense Braxton Hicks, especially in the evening.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Keep easy, high-energy snacks stocked and visible: trail mix, granola bars, bananas, cheese sticks, and crackers with peanut butter. When labor starts, your partner will want to eat lightly in early labor (if she feels like it), and YOU will need fuel too — labor can last a long time, and a hungry support person is a cranky support person. Toss a few snack bags into the hospital bag today.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Squats are surprisingly helpful this late in the game. They open the pelvic outlet, strengthen the legs for pushing, and can help baby descend. Your partner can try supported squats — holding onto a sturdy chair or the kitchen counter, feet shoulder-width apart, lowering down gently and holding for a few seconds. Even 5-10 gentle squats a day can make a difference. If squats feel like too much, simple hip circles while standing work great too.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Pack your own hospital bag today if you haven't already. Here's your checklist: phone charger (long cord!), snacks (nuts, granola bars, dried fruit — nothing smelly), a change of clothes, deodorant, toothbrush, a small pillow, entertainment for long waits (book, headphones), cash or card for vending machines or cafeteria, and a going-home outfit for baby. Pro tip: pack a swimsuit in case your partner wants you in the shower with her during labor for back support.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What's one tradition from your family — or a new one — you'd like to start with our baby?" Whether it's Friday night dinners, a special birthday ritual, or bedtime songs, talking about family traditions right now can make the future feel beautifully real.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Babies are born with about 300 bones — adults have only 206! Many of baby's bones are made of soft cartilage that will gradually fuse and harden over the first few years of life. This flexibility is what allows baby to make it through the birth canal.

## 🛏️ Sleep Tip
Your partner's sleep is probably rough, but here's something for YOU: start banking sleep now. Seriously. Go to bed 30 minutes earlier than usual this week. Once baby arrives, sleep becomes a rare commodity, and every extra hour you store up now is going to matter. Also, practice sleeping in short bursts — set an alarm for a 20-minute power nap this weekend. You'll need this skill soon.

## 💼 Workplace Tip
If you haven't already finalized your paternity leave plans, do it this week. Email your manager and HR with your expected start date and duration. Set up an out-of-office message draft so it's ready to activate at a moment's notice. Brief your colleagues on anything they'll need to handle in your absence. The more you prepare now, the more fully present you can be when baby arrives.
