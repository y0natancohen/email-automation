# Week 27, Day 5

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby now weighs about 2 pounds and is building up more body fat, which will help regulate temperature after birth. The skin is still a bit wrinkled (like the baby has been in a bath too long!) but is starting to smooth out as fat deposits increase. The lungs are producing more surfactant — the substance that keeps the air sacs from sticking together — which is a critical step toward being able to breathe independently.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Hormonal changes in the third trimester can bring a rollercoaster of emotions. Your partner might feel deeply connected to the baby one moment and overwhelmed by everything that needs to happen before birth the next. This is completely normal. She may also be experiencing vivid dreams — pregnancy hormones plus anxiety about the unknown can lead to some truly wild nighttime adventures. Reassure her that bizarre pregnancy dreams are extremely common.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Calcium is crucial now as your baby's bones are developing rapidly. If your partner isn't getting enough calcium, her body will actually pull it from her own bones to supply the baby. Good sources: yogurt, cheese, fortified orange juice, tofu, and leafy greens like kale. Aim for about 1,000 mg per day. A cup of yogurt with breakfast and some cheese at lunch can get you most of the way there.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Squats are a fantastic third-trimester exercise — they strengthen the legs and pelvic floor, and can help prepare the body for labor positions. Your partner can try:
- Wall squats: lean back against a wall, feet shoulder-width apart, slide down until thighs are parallel (or as far as comfortable), hold for 10 seconds
- Supported squats: holding onto a chair or countertop for balance
- Start with 5-10 reps and build up gradually

Offer to do them together — it's more fun (and harder than it looks!).

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Here's something many new dads don't expect: the emotional weight of the third trimester. You might find yourself worrying more — about finances, about being a good father, about the birth itself. These feelings are valid and completely normal. If you need to talk, reach out to a friend who's been through it, or consider joining an online community for expecting dads. You don't have to process everything alone.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What do you think our parenting strengths will be? Where do you think we'll complement each other?"

## 🎉 Fun Fact
At this stage, your baby responds to touch! If your partner presses gently on her belly, the baby might push back or shift position. It's one of the earliest forms of interaction between parents and baby. Try it tonight — place your hand on the belly and press very gently. You might get a little push or kick in response.

## 🤔 Weekly Q&A — Answer
**Wednesday's question:** When should we start thinking about a birth plan, and what should it include?

**Answer:** Now is actually a perfect time to start discussing it! A birth plan is simply a document that communicates your preferences to your medical team. It doesn't need to be complicated. Consider including: preferred pain management options, who you want in the room, preferences about movement during labor, thoughts on interventions, skin-to-skin contact immediately after birth, and feeding preferences. Remember — a birth plan is a wish list, not a contract. Things may change during labor, and flexibility is important. Start the conversation with your partner and your healthcare provider in the coming weeks.

## 🧠 Mental Health Check-In
The third trimester can trigger anxiety about birth, parenthood, and all the changes ahead. Both of you might feel it differently. Check in with each other: "How are you really feeling about everything?" Don't try to fix each other's worries — sometimes just being heard is enough. If anxiety feels overwhelming or persistent, don't hesitate to talk to a professional. Prenatal anxiety is common and very treatable.

## 💕 Intimacy Corner
Physical intimacy can look different in the third trimester — and that's okay. The growing belly means some positions might not work anymore, and your partner may have different comfort levels. But intimacy isn't just physical. Holding hands, giving foot rubs, cuddling on the couch, or even just sitting together in comfortable silence — these all count. Keep the physical connection alive in whatever way feels right for both of you. And for the record: sex is generally safe throughout pregnancy unless your doctor has said otherwise.
