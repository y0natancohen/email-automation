# Week 35, Day 2

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby's skull bones are developed but not yet fused together — and this is by design. The separate plates can overlap slightly during birth, allowing baby's head to fit through the birth canal. This is why some newborns have a slightly cone-shaped head at first. Don't worry — it rounds out within a few days. The soft spots (fontanelles) will remain open for months after birth to allow the brain to continue growing.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Frequent urination is probably at an all-time high. Your partner might feel like she just went to the bathroom and already needs to go again. Heartburn, back pain, and difficulty sleeping continue to be the not-so-fun trio of late pregnancy. On the emotional side, she might be experiencing a "nesting on steroids" urge — wanting everything to be perfect and ready. Let her nest, but make sure she's not lifting heavy things or climbing ladders.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Fiber is your partner's best friend right now. Constipation in late pregnancy is incredibly common due to hormones and the pressure of the uterus on the intestines. High-fiber foods: oatmeal, berries, pears, broccoli, lentils, and whole grain bread. Pair fiber with plenty of water — at least 8-10 glasses a day. Dried prunes are a natural and effective remedy if things are really backed up.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
**Exercise Focus: Perineal Massage**
Starting around 35-36 weeks, perineal massage can help prepare the tissues for stretching during birth and may reduce the risk of tearing. Your partner can do this herself or you can help. Using a natural oil (like olive or coconut oil), gently massage and stretch the perineal area for about 5-10 minutes, a few times per week. There are good video tutorials available from reputable birth education sources. It's not glamorous, but research supports its benefits for first-time moms.

Also: keep up those daily walks and pelvic floor exercises!

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Learn about the "golden hour" — the first hour after birth. During this time, baby is placed skin-to-skin on mom's chest (or on you, if mom is unable). This hour is crucial for bonding, regulating baby's temperature, and initiating breastfeeding. Knowing about it means you can advocate for it in the delivery room. Let your birth team know this is important to you.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What do we want the first hour after birth to look like? Who do we want in the room? What are our priorities?" Having this conversation now means you can communicate your wishes to your care team clearly.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Your baby is swallowing up to a liter of amniotic fluid per day. This helps develop the digestive system and contributes to the formation of meconium — baby's first poop, which will make its debut in the first day or two after birth. Fair warning: meconium is dark, sticky, and tar-like. It's normal, but it's... memorable.

## 🤔 Yesterday's Answer
**Group B Strep (GBS) — Here's the deal:**

GBS is a type of bacteria that about 25% of healthy women carry in their vaginal or rectal area. It's completely harmless to the mother but can potentially be passed to the baby during delivery. The concern is that in rare cases, it can cause infection in newborns.

**The test:** It's simple — a swab of the vagina and rectum, done between weeks 36-38. Results take a couple of days.

**If positive:** Your partner will receive IV antibiotics during labor (usually penicillin). That's it. The antibiotics are very effective at preventing transmission to the baby. She won't need to take antibiotics before labor starts — only during active labor.

**If negative:** No antibiotics needed during labor for GBS.

**Should you worry?** Not really. GBS is common, the test is easy, and the treatment is straightforward. It doesn't mean anything is wrong — it's just a standard screening to keep baby safe. Ask about the timing of this test at your next prenatal appointment.
