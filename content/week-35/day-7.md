# Week 35, Day 7

## 👶 Baby Development
By the end of this week, if your baby were born now, they would likely do very well with minimal medical intervention. At 35 weeks, most babies just need a little help regulating temperature and perhaps some support with feeding. The sucking and swallowing coordination is still maturing, which is why those last few weeks make such a difference. Every day in the womb counts.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Your partner might be feeling a mix of impatience and apprehension. The discomforts of pregnancy are stacking up, and there might be moments where she just wants it to be over — followed by anxiety about what "over" actually means (labor and delivery). Both feelings coexist, and both are completely valid. Physically, round ligament pain, back pain, and exhaustion continue to be daily companions.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Vitamin K plays an important role in blood clotting, which is crucial for both delivery and recovery. Good sources: leafy greens (kale, spinach, broccoli), Brussels sprouts, and fermented foods. A simple way to boost intake: add a handful of spinach to any smoothie — you won't taste it, but the nutrients are significant.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Rest day. Read a book, watch a show, or just close your eyes for 20 minutes. Your body is running a marathon even when you're sitting still. Honor that.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Download your hospital's patient app if they have one. Many hospitals now have apps that let you pre-register for delivery, view maps of the facility, and access your birth team's contact information. Pre-registration saves time and paperwork during the chaos of arrival. Also: save the labor and delivery unit's phone number in your phone right now.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What surprised you most about this pregnancy?" Reflecting together on the journey so far can be really meaningful when you're this close to the finish line.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Oxytocin — the "love hormone" — is what triggers labor contractions, promotes bonding after birth, and helps with milk let-down during breastfeeding. The same hormone is released during hugging, kissing, and cuddling. Nature's design is pretty elegant: the hormone of love literally brings your baby into the world.

## 🔮 After-Birth Preview: The First 24 Hours
Here's what to expect in the first day after your baby is born:

**In the delivery room (first 1-2 hours):**
- Baby is placed skin-to-skin on mom's chest immediately (golden hour)
- First breastfeeding attempt — baby has a strong instinct to find the breast
- Baby is weighed, measured, and given an Apgar score (a quick health assessment at 1 and 5 minutes after birth)
- Vitamin K shot and eye ointment are administered
- You'll cut the cord (if you want to — no pressure!)
- The placenta is delivered
- If there are tears, they'll be stitched

**The first 12 hours:**
- You'll move to a postpartum room
- Baby will likely want to feed frequently — this is normal and helps establish milk supply
- Nurses will check on mom and baby regularly (vitals, bleeding, uterine firmness)
- Baby will have their first diaper — meconium, the dark, tarry first poop
- You'll practice swaddling, diapering, and holding your tiny human
- Visitors may come (if you allow them — it's okay to say "not yet")
- Exhaustion will hit both of you like a wall

**What you should do:**
- Be the gatekeeper: manage visitors, hold the baby so mom can sleep, fetch water and snacks
- Take photos — but also put the phone down and just be present
- Ask questions — nurses are your biggest resource. No question is too basic
- Eat and sleep when you can — you're no good to anyone if you're running on empty

**Emotionally:**
- You might cry. You might not feel anything dramatic right away. Both are normal
- Love at first sight is real for some parents; for others, bonding takes time. Neither is wrong
- The surreal feeling of "I'm actually a parent now" might not fully land for days or weeks

## 🙏 Rest & Gratitude
Four weeks left (roughly). Take a mental snapshot of this moment — you as a couple, on the edge of everything changing. Whatever happens next, you're doing this together. That's the most important part.
