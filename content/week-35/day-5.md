# Week 35, Day 5

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby's brain weighs about two-thirds of what it will at 39-40 weeks. Those final weeks of brain growth are significant — it's one of the key reasons doctors encourage babies to stay in the womb until at least 39 weeks if everything is going well. Neural connections are forming at a staggering rate, laying the foundation for every thought, movement, and sensation your child will ever have.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Your partner might be experiencing "lightning crotch" more frequently — those sudden, sharp, shooting pains in the pelvis or vagina caused by baby pressing on nerves or the cervix. They're startling but harmless. She might also notice more frequent Braxton Hicks, especially after physical activity or when dehydrated. Emotionally, the reality of impending motherhood might feel both thrilling and terrifying.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Protein intake is important for the final stretch — baby is building muscle and gaining weight rapidly. Aim for lean proteins at every meal: eggs, chicken, fish, tofu, beans, or Greek yogurt. A quick high-protein snack: hard-boiled eggs prepped in advance (make a batch at the start of the week) with a pinch of salt and everything bagel seasoning.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Practice your breathing techniques together today. You'll use these during labor:

**For early labor:** Slow, deep breaths — in through the nose for 4 counts, out through the mouth for 6 counts. Focus on making the exhale longer than the inhale.

**For active labor:** Shorter, rhythmic breathing — in through the nose, out through the mouth in a steady pattern. Think "hee-hee-hooo" or whatever rhythm feels natural.

**For pushing:** Deep breath in, chin to chest, bear down while slowly releasing air.

Practice these for 5 minutes together. You'll be her guide and anchor during labor.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Here's something important: during labor, your partner may say things she doesn't mean, snap at you, or tell you not to touch her one minute and then ask you to hold her the next. This is completely normal. Labor is the most physically intense experience most people go through. Don't take anything personally. Your job is to be a steady, calm presence no matter what. Think of yourself as a lighthouse — unwavering, constant, and dependable.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"If we need to make quick decisions during labor, what are your non-negotiables?" Some women feel strongly about pain medication, birth positions, or who cuts the cord. Knowing her priorities helps you advocate for her when she can't.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
The average first-time labor lasts about 12-18 hours from start to finish, but it can range from a few hours to over 24. The longest part is usually early labor (the first stage), which many women spend at home. By the time you're at the hospital, things typically progress more quickly. But every labor is unique — some babies are in a hurry, others take their time.

## 🤔 Yesterday's Answer
**The stages of labor explained:**

**Stage 1: Cervix dilation (the longest part)**
- *Early labor:* Cervix dilates from 0 to about 6 cm. Contractions are mild to moderate, 5-15 minutes apart. Can last hours to days. Most of this happens at home. Walk, eat, rest, shower.
- *Active labor:* Cervix dilates from 6 to 10 cm. Contractions are intense, 3-5 minutes apart, lasting 45-60 seconds. This is usually when you head to the hospital. Pain management options kick in here.
- *Transition:* The final stretch from about 8-10 cm. The most intense part — contractions are very close together. Your partner may feel nauseous, shaky, and say she can't do it. This is actually a sign that baby is almost here. This phase is mercifully short, usually 30 minutes to 2 hours.

**Stage 2: Pushing and delivery**
- Begins at full dilation (10 cm). Your partner will feel an overwhelming urge to push. For first-time moms, pushing typically takes 30 minutes to 3 hours. This is the part where your coaching, encouragement, and presence matter most. Then — baby is born.

**Stage 3: Placenta delivery**
- The placenta delivers 5-30 minutes after baby. Your partner probably won't even notice this part because she'll be holding your baby.

**What you should know:** It won't go perfectly according to plan, and that's fine. Flexibility is key. The end goal is a healthy baby and a healthy mom, however that happens.

## 🧠 Mental Health Check
Five weeks out, and the weight of impending fatherhood might be hitting differently. Maybe you're wondering: Am I ready for this? What if I'm not a good dad? What if something goes wrong? These thoughts are universal among expectant fathers. The fact that you're worried about being a good parent is actually evidence that you will be one. Talk about these feelings — with your partner, a friend, a family member, or a therapist. You don't have to carry the emotional load alone.

## 💕 Intimacy
As the belly grows, physical closeness requires creativity but it doesn't require less love. Some ideas for staying connected: give your partner a shoulder and neck massage — pregnancy tension gathers there. Take a warm bath together (not too hot). Lie in bed facing each other and talk about your baby. If sexual intimacy feels complicated right now, remember that it's temporary. Focus on maintaining emotional intimacy and physical affection, and the rest will find its rhythm again after recovery.
