# Week 14, Day 2

## 👶 Baby Development
Lanugo is here. A fine, soft, downy hair is beginning to sprout across your baby's entire body — forehead, arms, back, legs, everywhere. This might sound strange, but it serves an important purpose: lanugo helps hold the vernix caseosa (a waxy, cheese-like coating that will develop soon) onto the skin. The vernix protects the baby's delicate skin from the amniotic fluid — imagine soaking in a bath for months; you'd need some protection too. Most lanugo is shed before birth (the baby actually swallows some of it, which becomes part of meconium), but some babies are born with traces of it, especially premature babies.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
The second trimester appetite is real. Your partner might feel genuinely hungry for the first time in weeks — and the food she wants might be very specific. Cravings can be intense: sour foods (pickles, citrus), salty snacks, comfort foods, or unusual combinations. As long as she's eating a balanced diet overall, indulging cravings is fine. She might also notice that she feels warmer than usual — the increased blood volume (up by about 45% by this point) generates extra heat. Light, breathable layers are her friend.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Quick tip: DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) is the specific omega-3 fatty acid most important for your baby's brain and eye development. The ideal intake during pregnancy is at least 200-300 mg of DHA per day. Best dietary sources:
- Salmon (1,200 mg DHA per 3-oz serving — a single serving covers you for days)
- Sardines (740 mg per 3-oz serving)
- Anchovies (1,300 mg per 3-oz serving)
- Fortified eggs (varies, check the label)
- If supplementing, look for a prenatal DHA supplement derived from algae or fish oil

Avoid high-mercury fish: shark, swordfish, king mackerel, and tilefish.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
**Prenatal Core Activation (10 minutes):**
Traditional core work (crunches, sit-ups) is off the table during pregnancy, but core strength is still crucial. Try these safe alternatives:
1. **Standing Pelvic Tilts** — Stand with back against a wall. Gently tilt the pelvis to flatten the lower back against the wall. Hold 5 seconds. Release. 15 reps.
2. **All-Fours Belly Breathing** — On hands and knees, inhale and let the belly drop slightly. Exhale and gently draw the belly button up (don't suck in — just engage gently). 10 reps.
3. **Pallof Press** — With a resistance band anchored to a doorknob, hold the handle at chest height and press arms straight out. The band will try to rotate you — resist it. 10 reps each side.

These exercises strengthen the transverse abdominis (the deep core muscle that wraps around the torso like a corset) without causing diastasis recti.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Start talking to the belly. Seriously. Your baby's hearing won't be fully functional until around week 18, but the vibrations of your voice travel through the amniotic fluid even now. Research shows that babies respond to familiar voices after birth — and your voice can become one of them. You don't need to give speeches. Just say hi, tell the baby about your day, read a book out loud, or sing a song (no talent required). Some dads feel silly doing this at first, but it often becomes one of their favorite parts of the pregnancy.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Here's the answer to yesterday's lanugo question: **Some babies are born with lanugo still present.** This is especially common in babies born early (before 37 weeks), but it can happen with full-term babies too. When present at birth, lanugo is usually found on the shoulders, back, ears, and forehead. It typically falls off within a few weeks — you might notice fine hairs on the crib sheet. It's completely harmless and normal. Some cultures consider a baby born with lanugo to be extra lucky.

## 🏋️ Exercise Focus: Why Balance Training Matters
As the belly grows and the center of gravity shifts forward, balance becomes more challenging. The hormone relaxin is also loosening ligaments, making joints less stable. This combination increases the risk of falls during pregnancy.

Balance exercises to try:
- **Single Leg Stand** — Stand near a wall for safety. Lift one foot off the floor and balance for 20-30 seconds. Switch. As this gets easy, try closing your eyes (with the wall nearby).
- **Tandem Walking** — Walk heel-to-toe in a straight line, as if on a tightrope. 20 steps.
- **Stability Ball Sit** — Sit on a stability ball and lift one foot off the floor for 10 seconds. Alternate.

Practicing balance now reduces fall risk in the third trimester when the belly is significantly larger.
