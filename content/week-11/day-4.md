# Week 11, Day 4

## 👶 Baby Development
Your baby's hands are becoming more sophisticated by the day. Those tiny fingers can now open wide and close into fists. The movements are still reflexive rather than intentional, but the motor pathways between the brain and muscles are forming rapidly. The palms are developing sensitivity to touch — if you could gently brush the palm, the fingers would curl around yours. This grasp reflex will be one of the first things you experience when your newborn grabs your finger for the first time. Your baby's body is also becoming more proportional — the head is still large but now makes up about half the body length, down from about two-thirds a few weeks ago.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Your partner's sense of balance might feel slightly off. The growing uterus is starting to shift her center of gravity, and hormonal changes are loosening ligaments and joints (especially in the pelvis, preparing for birth). She might feel more clumsy or unsteady than usual. This isn't imagination — it's physiology. Good supportive shoes become more important than ever. She may also notice that she's more emotional at certain times of day — many women report the evening being the hardest, when fatigue compounds the hormonal shifts.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
Quick tip: Iodine is an unsung hero of pregnancy nutrition. It's essential for your baby's brain development and thyroid function. Most people get iodine from iodized salt, dairy products, eggs, and fish. The pregnancy requirement is 220 micrograms per day (compared to 150 for non-pregnant adults). Many prenatal vitamins contain iodine, but not all — check the label. If your partner uses mainly sea salt or kosher salt for cooking, those typically aren't iodized, so it's worth being aware.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
**Wall Push-Ups (great for pregnancy):**
Stand facing a wall, about arm's length away. Place hands flat on the wall at shoulder height. Bend elbows to bring your chest toward the wall, then push back. Do 3 sets of 12. This maintains upper body strength without putting pressure on the abdomen. Stronger arms and shoulders will be incredibly useful for carrying, feeding, and soothing a newborn — your baby may start at 7 pounds, but they keep getting heavier.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Write down three questions for the next prenatal appointment. Having questions prepared shows your partner (and the doctor) that you're engaged. Some good ones for this stage:
- "When should we schedule the first trimester screening?"
- "Are there any additional tests you'd recommend given our ages and history?"
- "What warning signs should we watch for?"
Going in prepared turns a passive waiting room experience into an active partnership moment.

## 💬 Conversation Starter
"What do you think will be the hardest adjustment when the baby comes? And what do you think will come naturally?" Be honest with each other. The goal isn't to have all the answers — it's to know you're both thinking about it.

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Your baby can already hiccup, and it's happening regularly. Hiccups at this stage are caused by sudden contractions of the diaphragm as it develops. They're practice for the breathing movements your baby will need after birth. You can't feel them yet, but later in pregnancy, you'll recognize fetal hiccups as a rhythmic, repetitive tapping in your partner's belly — different from the random movements of kicks and rolls.

## 🔬 Deep Dive: The Nuchal Translucency (NT) Scan
If you haven't had it yet, the NT scan is typically performed between 11 weeks + 5 days and 13 weeks + 6 days. Here's what you need to know:
- **What it is:** A specialized ultrasound that measures the fluid-filled space at the back of the baby's neck (the nuchal fold).
- **What it measures:** All babies have some fluid here, but an increased thickness may indicate a higher risk of chromosomal conditions like Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) or heart defects.
- **How it works:** Combined with a blood test (measuring levels of free beta-hCG and PAPP-A) and maternal age, it gives a risk estimate.
- **Important to know:** This is a screening, not a diagnostic test. It tells you the probability, not the answer. A "high-risk" result means further testing (like amniocentesis or CVS) may be offered.
- **The experience:** You'll see your baby on the ultrasound screen — moving, perhaps waving. For many couples, this is the most detailed look at the baby so far, and it can be deeply emotional.

Talk to your OB about scheduling this if you haven't already.
