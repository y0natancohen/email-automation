# Week 22, Day 3

## 👶 Baby Development
If you could see your baby right now, you'd be amazed at how much they look like an actual newborn — just smaller and thinner. The facial features are all in place: eyebrows, eyelids, lips, nose, ears. The proportions are becoming more balanced as the body catches up to the head size. Your baby's lungs are developing structures called bronchioles (tiny airways), but they won't be functional for breathing air until much later. Inside the lungs, a substance called surfactant is just beginning to form — this will eventually keep the air sacs from collapsing when the baby breathes for the first time.

## 🤰 What Mom Might Be Feeling
Your partner might be experiencing vivid dreams. Pregnancy hormones, combined with more frequent waking (thanks to bladder pressure), mean she's more likely to remember her dreams. Many pregnant women report dreams about the baby, about labor, or bizarre scenarios that make no logical sense. These are normal and usually reflect the brain processing the huge life change ahead. She might also notice increased hair growth — not just on her head, but potentially on her belly, face, or arms. Blame the hormones; it's all temporary.

## 🍽️ Nutrition Tip
**DHA smoothie for brain building:** Blend together 1 cup of milk (or fortified plant milk), 1/2 cup frozen mango, 1 tablespoon chia seeds, 1 tablespoon ground flaxseed, and a handful of spinach. The chia and flax provide plant-based omega-3s, the mango adds sweetness and vitamin C, and the spinach sneaks in iron and folate. Quick to make, easy to drink, and packed with brain-building nutrients. Add a scoop of protein powder if she needs an extra boost.

## 💪 Exercise & Movement
Today's movement tip is about posture awareness. As the belly grows, many women unconsciously lean backward to counterbalance, which strains the lower back. Encourage your partner to:
- Stand tall with shoulders back and down
- Keep ears aligned over shoulders, shoulders over hips
- Tuck the pelvis slightly to reduce the lower back curve
- When sitting, use a small pillow behind the lower back

Good posture now prevents a lot of pain later.

## 💡 Tip for Dad
Start thinking about your birth support role. You don't need to decide everything now, but begin exploring what kind of birth partner you want to be. Do you want to be at the head of the bed, holding her hand and whispering encouragement? Do you want to see everything? Are you comfortable with the idea of cutting the cord? There are no right answers — just your answers. Talking about this early prevents awkward in-the-moment decisions.

## 💬 Conversation Starter

## 🎉 Fun Fact
Your baby's lungs won't take their first real breath until birth, but they're already "practicing" by making breathing-like movements with the chest muscles. The diaphragm contracts and relaxes rhythmically, moving amniotic fluid in and out. It's like rehearsal for opening night.

## 🛏️ Sleep Tip
If your partner is having trouble falling back asleep after nighttime bathroom trips (which are increasing), try this: keep the path to the bathroom dimly lit with a nightlight rather than turning on overhead lights. Bright light triggers the brain to wake up fully, while dim light allows the body to stay in a sleepy state. Also, a few sips of water but not a full glass before bed can reduce the number of trips. And if she's wide awake? A boring podcast (like a monotone history lecture) at low volume can help the brain settle back down.

## 💼 Workplace Tip
If you haven't already, consider telling your employer about the pregnancy. At 22 weeks, it's typically a good time — you're well past the first trimester, things feel solid, and you'll need to start planning for your time off. Frame it positively: you're excited, you want to plan ahead to ensure a smooth transition, and you want to discuss what your paternity leave options look like. In Israel, fathers are entitled to take part of the maternity leave if both parents agree. Know your rights before the conversation.
